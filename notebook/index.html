Section 01

Why asking AI for answers is the wrong move.

Open a blank Claude conversation and ask it to review your business strategy. It will give you a thorough, balanced, thoughtful response that agrees with most of what you said and gently flags a few things to consider.

That response is useless.

Not because Claude is bad at strategy — it isn't. But because a blank Claude has no idea who you are, what standards you hold yourself to, what you've already tried, or what kind of feedback actually helps you think. It's responding to your words, not to you. So it defaults to the safest, most agreeable version of whatever you asked.

The core problem

A blank AI is optimised to be helpful in the generic sense. Your job is to make it helpful in the specific sense — for your business, your voice, your standards. That's what this notebook teaches you to do.

The blank slate problem

Every time you open a new conversation, Claude starts fresh. It doesn't remember that you hate corporate jargon, that you've already tried the obvious LinkedIn strategy, that your clients are senior finance professionals who will see through anything that sounds like a template.

This isn't a flaw — it's a design choice. The problem is that most people never solve it. They keep prompting a blank slate and then wondering why the outputs feel generic.

The solution isn't a better prompt. It's a configured AI — one that runs with a set of instructions before every conversation even begins. Those instructions are called skills.

What this notebook is not

This is not a prompt library. Collecting prompts is like collecting recipes without learning to cook — you'll always be dependent on someone else's version. This notebook teaches you to build the system, so the prompts you need emerge naturally from how you've configured your AI.

Section 03

What a skill actually is — in plain English.

Forget the technical framing. A skill is a set of instructions that runs before every conversation. That's it.

When you upload a skill to Claude, those instructions become part of how Claude thinks throughout the session. It's not a prompt you type — it's a configuration that's already active when you open the chat. Like hiring someone who's already been briefed, rather than starting from scratch every time.

The basic structure

A skill file is a plain text document with a .md extension — standard Markdown. Claude reads it as a structured set of instructions. Here's the skeleton every skill is built on:

Skill file structure — template
---
name: your-skill-name
description: One sentence on what this skill does
---

# Who you are
You are [persona / role / context].
You serve [who you work with and what they need].

# How you think
Your approach is [methodology / framework / standards].
You always [core behaviour 1].
You always [core behaviour 2].

# What you must always do
- [Non-negotiable behaviour A]
- [Non-negotiable behaviour B]
- [Non-negotiable behaviour C]

# What you must never do
- [Hard boundary 1]
- [Hard boundary 2]

That's the full structure. The sophistication isn't in the format — it's in the quality of the answers you put into it. Section 05 covers exactly what those answers should be.

How to upload a skill to Claude

Save your skill file as skill-name.md. In Claude, go to Projects → create a new project → upload the file under Project Instructions. Every conversation you start inside that project runs with your skill active automatically.

One skill, one purpose

Build separate skills for separate contexts. A skill for writing LinkedIn content shouldn't also be your research verification skill — the instructions will conflict. Keep them focused, and load the right one for the task at hand.

Section 04

Build your first skill — step by step.

This takes 30 minutes. You'll need a text editor (Notes, Word, Google Docs — anything that saves plain text) and a Claude account. Follow these five steps in order.

Step 1 — Answer the five questions

Before you write a single line of the skill file, answer these questions in plain language. Don't overthink them. You're not writing for an audience — you're briefing an AI:

Who are you? Your role, your business, what you actually do day to day.
Who do you serve? Your clients or audience — their profile, their sophistication, what they care about.
How do you think? Your methodology, your standards, what makes your work different from the generic version.
What must this AI always do? The non-negotiables — cite sources, push back on weak claims, use your tone, whatever matters most.
What must this AI never do? The hard lines — no corporate jargon, no unsourced statistics, no passive voice, whatever you can't stand.

Step 2 — Write the skill file

Open a new document. Use the template from Section 03. Fill in each section using your answers from Step 1. Write in plain English, in the second person — "You are...", "You always...", "You never...". Claude reads instructions best when they're direct and specific.

Step 3 — Package it

Save the file with a .md extension — for example, linkedin-writing.md. If you have multiple skills, put them in a folder and zip it.

Step 4 — Upload to Claude

In Claude.ai: go to Projects in the left sidebar → New Project → give it a name → under Project Instructions, upload your skill file. Every conversation you start in this project will run with your skill active.

Step 5 — Test with a real conversation

Don't test with a toy example. Use something you'd actually ask — a real piece of content you need to write, a real strategic question you're sitting on. Notice where the AI's response feels different from what a blank Claude would give you. That difference is the skill working.

If the output still feels generic

Your instructions are probably too vague. "Be professional" means nothing. "Write at a senior executive register, no bullet points in persuasive sections, always lead with the reader's problem not your solution" — that means something. Go back to the skill file and make the instructions more specific.

Section 05

The five questions every useful skill must answer.

These aren't just the five questions from Step 1 — they're the diagnostic framework for any skill that doesn't perform the way you expected. If a skill produces output that feels off, it's almost always because one of these five questions wasn't answered specifically enough.

  • 1

    Who am I?

    The AI needs a role and a context, not just a job title. "Marketing consultant" is not enough. "A Hong Kong-born marketing consultant operating across London and HK, working primarily with SME owners who are technically literate but marketing-novice" — that's a briefing.

    Bad: "You are a marketing expert." Good: "You are a senior marketing consultant with 10+ years in Martech, advising SME founders who are growth-focused but time-poor."

  • 2

    Who do I serve?

    The AI needs to understand your audience as specifically as you do. Their sophistication level, their vocabulary, their objections, what they respond to. This shapes every sentence it writes on your behalf.

    Bad: "Business owners." Good: "Established SME owners in London and HK who've outgrown generic advice and respond to specificity, data, and directness — not inspiration."

  • 3

    How do I think?

    Your methodology. Your framework. How you approach a problem differently from the default. This is the hardest question to answer well — and the most valuable to get right. If you have a proprietary approach, encode it here.

    Bad: "I use a strategic approach." Good: "I apply the REAL framework — Research, Evaluate, Automate, Long-lasting. Every recommendation must map to a stage."

  • 4

    What must this always do?

    The non-negotiables. These are the behaviours that define quality in your context. Be specific enough that violating them would produce something you'd be embarrassed to send.

    Bad: "Always be professional." Good: "Always cite the source of any statistic. Always distinguish between a growth rate and an absolute figure. Flag any claim that relies on a single study."

  • 5

    What must this never do?

    The hard lines. Things that would make you immediately distrust the output. Often these are stylistic — passive voice, corporate filler, excessive hedging — or substantive: never recommend a tool you haven't tested, never lead with price.

    Bad: "Don't be generic." Good: "Never use the phrases 'leverage', 'synergy', or 'game-changer'. Never open a post with 'I' as the first word. Never present a recommendation without a reason."

Section 06

What a professional AI toolkit looks like at the ceiling.

The skill you built in Section 04 is a foundation. It handles one context well. A professional toolkit has multiple skills working together — each optimised for a specific domain — so your AI performs at a high level whether you're writing content, doing research, preparing for a client meeting, or managing your pipeline.

Here's what that looks like in practice. These are the four capability domains where a configured AI makes the biggest difference for SME owners and professionals:

Research verification

Flags weak claims, distinguishes data from inference, identifies single-study figures, surfaces conflicting sources. Stops you from publishing something that a well-informed reader will immediately distrust.

Platform intelligence

Verifies current algorithm behaviour before writing any social content. What worked on LinkedIn six months ago may actively suppress reach today. A skill that checks before it writes keeps you current without the maintenance.

Production standards

Applies your voice, your format rules, your rationale requirements, and your quality bar consistently across every piece of output — whether you're writing a post, a proposal, or a client email.

Source-lock mode

Limits the AI's reference to documents you provide — your client brief, your proposal, your research. Prevents the AI from filling gaps with inference. Critical for client-facing work where accuracy is non-negotiable.

Building these yourself vs. starting from a professional set

You can build each of these from scratch using the framework in this notebook. Each takes 30–60 minutes to build well and several iterations to refine. Most professionals find that the research verification and platform intelligence skills are the hardest to get right — they require specificity about what counts as a credible source, how to handle conflicting data, and what the current platform rules actually are.
